# "Why San Francisco Can't Build Its Way Out" — Storyboard

**Format:** Vox-style explainer | **Target Length:** ~3:10 | **Resolution:** 1920x1080 @ 30fps

## Narration Script

### SCENE 1: THE HOOK (20s / 600 frames)
"The median home in San Francisco costs one-point-three million dollars. For that price, you could buy five houses in Houston, or an entire city block in Detroit. But in SF, it gets you a two-bedroom fixer-upper. How did one of America's most innovative cities become one of its most unaffordable? The answer isn't just about tech money — it's about a system designed to say no."

**Visual:** StatCard — "$1.3M" median home price

### SCENE 2: HOW WE GOT HERE (30s / 900 frames)
"The roots go back to nineteen seventy-eight, when California passed Proposition Thirteen, capping property tax increases and starving cities of revenue from new development. Then came Costa-Hawkins in ninety-five, limiting rent control. But the real acceleration began with tech. Between twenty-ten and twenty-nineteen, San Francisco added a hundred and ninety thousand jobs — but only thirty thousand housing units. The math simply doesn't work. For every six new workers, the city built one new home."

**Visual:** AnimatedTimeline — 1978 Prop 13, 1995 Costa-Hawkins, 1998 dot-com, 2008 crash, 2012 tech boom, 2020 pandemic, 2024 rebound

### SCENE 3: SUPPLY VS DEMAND (25s / 750 frames)
"California's own housing department says San Francisco needs to build eighty-two thousand new units by twenty thirty-one — about ten thousand a year. The city actually builds between two and four thousand. That gap between supply and demand is what drives prices up, pushes families out, and keeps the crisis grinding forward."

**Visual:** AnimatedBarChart — Jobs Added vs Housing Units Built by year (2010-2024)

### SCENE 4: THE MACHINE THAT SAYS NO (30s / 900 frames)
"Here's where it gets absurd. Thirty-eight percent of San Francisco's land was zoned exclusively for single-family homes. Even if a developer wants to build apartments near a transit station, any single neighbor can trigger a discretionary review that delays the project for months. Then there's CEQA — the California Environmental Quality Act — originally designed to protect nature, now routinely weaponized to block apartment buildings. The average time from proposal to permit? Two to four years. In Tokyo, it's six months."

**Visual:** Custom scene — flowchart/Rube Goldberg machine of the permitting process

### SCENE 5: THE TOKYO COMPARISON (25s / 750 frames)
"And that brings us to the most striking comparison in global housing. Tokyo — a city of fourteen million people — builds roughly a hundred and forty thousand housing units every year, because Japan sets zoning rules nationally, not neighborhood by neighborhood. The result? Despite growing population, Tokyo rents have barely budged in thirty years. San Francisco, with under a million people, can't manage three thousand units."

**Visual:** ComparisonChart — SF ~3K units/yr vs Tokyo ~140K units/yr

### SCENE 6: THE HUMAN COST (25s / 750 frames)
"The human toll is staggering. You need to earn over a hundred and thirty thousand dollars a year just to afford the median rent without being cost-burdened. Eight thousand three hundred people are unhoused — and sixty percent of them say housing cost is the reason. San Francisco's Black population has fallen from thirteen percent in nineteen-seventy to just five percent today. And here's the cruel irony: it now costs over seven hundred thousand dollars per unit to build subsidized affordable housing in the city — more than market-rate homes cost in most of America."

**Visual:** StatCard sequence — $130K income needed, 8,300 unhoused, Black population decline bar chart, $700K/unit subsidized cost

### SCENE 7: SIGNS OF CHANGE (20s / 600 frames)
"But Sacramento is finally pushing back. Senate Bill Nine now allows duplexes on most single-family lots statewide. SB Ten lets cities upzone near transit. And the builder's remedy — a nuclear option — lets developers bypass local zoning entirely if a city hasn't met its housing plan. The question isn't whether San Francisco knows how to fix this. It's whether it has the political will to actually say yes."

**Visual:** AnimatedTimeline — SB 9 (2021), SB 10 (2021), AB 2097 (2022), Builder's Remedy

### SCENE 8: THE CLOSE (15s / 450 frames)
"San Francisco has more dogs than children — a statistic that tells you everything about who can afford to put down roots. The city that built the Golden Gate Bridge now struggles to approve a duplex. The tools to fix this exist. The only thing missing is permission to use them."

**Visual:** Title card with closing text + sources
